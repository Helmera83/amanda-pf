# Design Hacks

Status: In Review
Category: Business
Tags: Design, Tutorial
Author: Amanda Helmer
Publication Date: November 30, 2025 6:00 PM (CST)

Improving product design as a non-designer starts with a few repeatable habits: talk to users often, simplify flows, and apply basic visual rules like consistency, spacing, and hierarchy. You do not need to “be a designer” to ship clearer, more usable products if you focus on small, high-leverage changes in how screens and journeys are structured.

## **Start with users, not features.**

Schedule short, frequent user conversations to see how people actually use your product, then prioritize problems they hit repeatedly (e.g., confusion, errors, drop-offs).

Map key journeys (signup, first value, core task) as simple step flows and remove or merge any step that does not clearly move the user toward success. 

Make tasks easy and learnable For each screen, ask: “What is the one thing the user should do here?” and make that action the most obvious button or control.

Reduce cognitive load by limiting choices, using clear labels instead of jargon, and keeping related actions grouped together in the interface.

Apply a few visual principles Use a small, consistent palette (1–2 main colors plus neutrals) and 2–3 text styles (e.g., heading, body, caption) across the product to avoid visual noise.

Improve readability by adding generous spacing between sections, aligning content to a grid, and using visual hierarchy (size, weight, and contrast) to show what matters most.

Design for feedback and errors Ensure every user action gets a clear response: button states, success messages, progress indicators, and inline error messages that explain what to do next.

Prevent mistakes where possible (validation, confirmations for destructive actions) and make recovery obvious with undo, back, or clear escape routes.

## **Use tools, patterns, and critique**

Stand on the shoulders of existing UI patterns and design systems from successful products instead of inventing from scratch; adapt proven layouts and flows to your context.

Work in simple tools (like templates or beginner‑friendly design apps) to mock up changes, then share early drafts with teammates or users and iterate based on specific feedback, not taste.
